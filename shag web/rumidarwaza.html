RUMI DARWAZA : रूमी दरवाज़ा : رومی دروازه

The Rumi Darwaza (sometimes known as the Turkish Gate), in Lucknow, Uttar Pradesh, India, is an imposing gateway which was built by Nawab Asaf-Ud-Daula in 1784. It is an example of Awadhi architecture. The Rumi Darwaza, which stands sixty feet tall, was modeled after the Sublime Porte (Bab-iHümayun) in Istanbul


It is adjacent to the Asafi Imambara, Teele Wali Masjid in Lucknow and has become a logo for the city of Lucknow. It used to mark the entrance to Old Lucknow. When the city grew and expanded, it was later used as an entrance to a palace which was later demolished by the British Raj following the Indian Mutiny.

The Rumi Darwaza is a fine example of Awadhi architecture, blending elements of Persian, Awadhi, and Mughal styles. Standing at a height of 60 feet, the gateway is an intricately designed structure with ornate embellishments. Its colossal arched doorway is crowned by an eight-sided chhatri, or umbrella-like structure, which adds to its regal charm.
Beyond its architectural significance, the Rumi Darwaza has historical importance as well. It is said to have served as a gateway for commoners during the day and as a royal entrance for the Nawabs in the evening. The Rumi Darwaza has withstood the test of time and stands today as a symbol of Lucknow's rich cultural heritage.

Lit up beautifully at night, the Rumi Darwaza becomes a captivating sight and a popular destination for locals and tourists alike. It continues to be a prominent landmark in Lucknow, connecting the present with the city's glorious past.
